Painex Clinic: 742,806 views in three months.

How a Pune pain-management clinic moved from a quiet Instagram presence to 367,000 unique accounts reached — with 98.9% of that reach coming from people who weren't already followers.

The lever was a hook-led reels strategy across three deliberate content lanes — condition explainers, day-in-the-life, and patient stories — with multilingual captions tuned for Pune's English-Hindi-Marathi audience.

The starting point

Painex is a Pune-based pain-management clinic with strong word-of-mouth and a small but engaged Instagram presence. The brief: lift discovery materially, without paid promotion, in a category (chronic pain, treatment specifics) where Instagram penalises clinical content if it gets the tone wrong.

What we changed

1. We picked three lanes and held them ruthlessly

Most healthcare Instagram accounts try to do everything — testimonials, doctor education, before/after, awareness day posts, festival greetings, team birthdays. The feed becomes noise. We held three:

  • Condition explainers — short, plain-language breakdowns of common pain conditions (sciatica, frozen shoulder, lumbar disc, knee osteoarthritis)
  • Day-in-the-life — humanising the practitioners, showing the clinic, the equipment, the patient journey
  • Patient stories — anonymised where required, real-voice where consent allowed, structured as "before → what we did → after"

2. Hooks designed for 1.2 seconds

Every reel opened on a pattern interrupt. For condition explainers — a specific symptom that the viewer was probably ignoring. For day-in-the-life — a frame that didn't look like a doctor's feed (movement, equipment, sound). For patient stories — a one-line specific symptom-claim from the patient.

3. Multilingual captions, written in Pune

The audience is Pune. The audience speaks English, Hindi, and Marathi — often in the same sentence. We wrote captions that code-switched the way Punekars actually talk. A condition explainer about back pain reads naturally in any of the three languages. This was the single largest contributor to 98.9% non-follower reach.

4. CapCut editing at a 1.8-second cut cadence

Healthcare content typically sits on a single shot for too long. We rebuilt the edit to cut every 1.8 seconds — sometimes with a B-roll insert, sometimes with a zoom punch-in. Watch-through climbed; the algorithm rewarded it.

5. Weekly publishing rhythm

Four reels a week, posted at proven local windows (lunch and post-dinner). One shoot day a month at the clinic, all raw footage captured in one block.

"98.9% of reach from non-followers is the metric that tells you the algorithm is actually doing the distribution — not your existing audience."

What the numbers actually mean

742,806 views and 367,000 reached are surface metrics. The compound business effect was different: practitioner-name recognition lifted in cold consultations, referral conversations got easier, and the clinic's phone calls began including "I saw your reel about [specific condition]" mentions for the first time. That is the real outcome — Instagram producing recall that travels offline.

Lessons portable to other Pune healthcare brands

  1. Pick three lanes, not five. Most clinics dilute themselves.
  2. Multilingual is not optional in Pune. Code-switched captions outperform single-language ones.
  3. The clinical accuracy bar is non-negotiable. Every reel went through second-pair-of-eyes review before posting.
  4. Hooks > production values. Phone-shot reels with strong hooks beat polished agency reels with weak ones.
  5. Consistency > intensity. Four reels a week for 12 weeks beats sixteen reels in week one and silence after.

Could we replicate this for your clinic?

If you have a clinician who can give us 4 hours on a shoot day once a month, and you trust us to write captions that match your clinical voice, yes — the system is portable. Specialty matters less than commitment to the publishing rhythm.
